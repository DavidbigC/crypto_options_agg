Volatility Trading — Plain English

A breakdown of jargon and strategies from the conversation between Jeff Liang & Grok

What's this conversation about?
Jeff asked Grok to reveal the "insider secret" of volatility trading. Grok's response: the core ideas are public knowledge — the real edge market makers have is in execution speed, order flow data, and proprietary infrastructure. The conversation then deep-dives into specific strategies and whether the "premiums" they exploit are sustainable.
01 — The Core Idea: Volatility Trading
What is "trading volatility"? Concept

Normal options trading is betting on price direction — will BTC go up or down? Volatility trading is different: you're betting on how much the price will move, not which way.

The key insight is that options have a built-in "fear price" called Implied Volatility (IV). If IV is too high or too low compared to what actually happens, there's a profit opportunity.

Analogy: Imagine a weather insurance company. They price insurance based on their forecast of storms. If they forecast 10 storms but only 6 happen, they overcharged — that's your profit if you're on the other side of that bet.
IV vs RV — The Core Trade Strategy

IV (Implied Volatility) = the market's expectation of future volatility, baked into option prices right now.

RV (Realized Volatility) = the volatility that actually happened over a period.

The trade: Sell options when IV is much higher than RV — you're selling "expensive fear". Historically, IV tends to run above RV in most markets (the "volatility risk premium"), meaning option sellers collect this gap over time.

In BTC, IV is often elevated because of crash risk and retail demand for protection. Selling that inflated IV (while delta-hedging the direction) is the core vol seller strategy.
02 — Strategies Mentioned
Straddle & Strangle Strategy

Both are ways to trade volatility without caring about direction.

Straddle: Buy (or sell) a call AND a put at the same strike price. If you buy it, you profit if BTC makes a big move either way. If you sell it, you profit if BTC stays flat and IV drops.

Strangle: Same idea, but the call and put are at different strikes (OTM). Cheaper to buy, but needs an even bigger move to profit.

Analogy: A straddle is like betting "something big will happen" at a boxing match. You don't care who wins — you just need fireworks. Selling a straddle is like being the casino: you make money if the fight is boring.
Gamma Scalping Strategy

This is a dynamic trading strategy used by market makers. When you own options (are "long gamma"), your delta changes as price moves. Gamma scalping means repeatedly rebalancing your hedge as price moves to lock in small profits from those movements.

You're essentially extracting value from price movement itself, as long as the actual moves are large enough to cover the cost of holding the options (theta decay).

Analogy: Imagine you're a market maker holding a straddle. BTC rises 3% — your position is now slightly long, so you sell a bit of BTC to re-neutralize. BTC drops back — you buy. Each "rebalance" captures a small profit. If BTC moves enough, these add up.
Calendar Spread Strategy

Buy an option with one expiry, sell the same strike option with a different expiry. The bet is on how the term structure of volatility will change — will near-term IV rise relative to far-term, or fall?

In contango (normal upward-sloping term structure), far-dated options have higher IV. You can sell near-term vol and buy far-term, profiting if the structure normalizes.

Risk Reversal (RR) Strategy

Buy an OTM call and sell an OTM put (or vice versa) at the same expiry. The price difference between them reflects the skew — how much the market fears downside vs upside.

When the skew is unusually steep (puts very expensive vs calls), you can do a reverse risk reversal: sell the expensive puts, buy cheap calls. You're betting the skew will normalize.

In BTC, when short-term skew is above the 90th historical percentile (very steep = very expensive puts), a reverse RR is the signal mentioned in the conversation.
03 — Volatility Surface Concepts
Volatility Smile / Skew Concept

If you plot IV against strike prices, it forms a curve — not flat. This curve is the "smile" (or smirk). Typically, OTM puts (downside protection) are more expensive than OTM calls, creating a skewed curve.

Why? Markets fear crashes more than rallies. Institutions buy put protection constantly, bidding up their IV. This is the "crash risk premium" embedded in the vol surface.

Analogy: Car insurance is like a put option. Everyone wants it, so it's "expensive" relative to the actual probability of an accident. The vol smile is just showing you where the fear is priced in.
Term Structure & Contango Concept

Term structure = how IV changes across different expiry dates (1 week, 1 month, 3 months, etc.).

Contango = far-dated IV is higher than near-dated IV. This is the normal state ("forward premium") — markets expect more uncertainty over longer horizons.

Backwardation = the opposite — near-term IV spikes above long-term (usually happens during a crisis/crash when near-term fear is extreme).

In bull markets, BTC tends to stay in contango. Selling near-term vol while buying far-term (calendar spread) can capture the "carry" from this structure.
Vol Surface Skew Arbitrage Strategy

This is what the conversation calls the "insider secret." The idea: model the entire volatility surface using a mathematical model (SABR or SVI), then look for relative mispricings — places where a specific strike/expiry has IV that's out of line with the rest of the surface.

For example: if 1-week 20-delta puts are priced at much higher IV than the model suggests, you sell them and hedge with something else on the surface that balances the risk.

This is NOT simple arbitrage — there's no guaranteed profit. The "mispricing" can persist or worsen. The edge is statistical and regime-dependent (~40-60% win rate, per Grok).
04 — Greeks (Beyond the Basics)
Delta
How much your option position moves when the underlying price moves $1. Delta-hedging = keeping this near zero so you're not betting on direction.
Gamma
How fast your delta changes as price moves. Long gamma = you benefit from big moves. Short gamma = you get hurt by them. Market makers manage this carefully.
Vega
How much your position profits/loses when IV changes by 1%. Long vol positions have positive vega; selling options gives you negative vega.
Theta
Time decay — how much value your option loses per day just from time passing. Option sellers collect theta; buyers pay it.
Vanna
How your delta changes when IV changes. Important for skew hedging — if IV spikes, your delta can shift unexpectedly, requiring a rebalance.
Volga
How your vega changes when IV changes (the "gamma of vega"). If IV moves a lot, your exposure to further IV moves changes — volga captures this.
05 — Models Mentioned
SABR Model Quant Model

A mathematical model for fitting the vol smile across strikes. "SABR" stands for Stochastic Alpha Beta Rho — the parameters that shape the curve. Market makers calibrate this model to live market prices constantly to see where the surface "should be" vs where it actually is.

SVI Model Quant Model

Stochastic Volatility Inspired — a simpler, more practical parameterization of the vol smile. Often used for fitting BTC option surfaces because it's faster to calibrate and avoids arbitrage by construction.

Sticky Delta vs Sticky Strike Regime

Two different assumptions about how the vol surface moves when the underlying price moves:

Sticky Strike: Each strike keeps its IV fixed as price moves. Simple assumption.

Sticky Delta: Each delta level (e.g., 25-delta put) keeps its IV fixed. As price moves, the whole surface "slides" with it. More realistic for most markets.

Knowing which regime you're in changes your hedging math — wrong assumption = wrong hedge.

06 — The Big Question: Are These Premiums Lasting?
Jeff's Question & Grok's Honest Answer

Jeff kept pushing: "Is the skew premium persistent? Is contango persistent?" The honest answer Grok gave:

No — not as risk-free arbitrage. If a guaranteed profit existed in the vol surface, market makers would immediately trade it away.

However, there are systematic biases in BTC options:

  • Crash risk keeps put skew persistently elevated
  • Bull markets keep the term structure in contango
  • Order flow imbalances create temporary mispricings
These give you 40-60% win-rate opportunities — not a money printer. They're regime-dependent. In crashes, contango inverts and skew explodes against you.
07 — Why Market Makers Win (Edge Is in Execution, Not Ideas)
The Real Moat

The conversation concludes that ~90% of the knowledge is public (books, papers, GitHub). The actual edge market makers have:

Factor Market Maker Retail Trader
Knowledge (SABR, vol surface) Public Accessible
Order flow data Real-time, proprietary No access
Execution speed Millisecond colocation Seconds of latency
Trading fees Near-zero (maker rebates) Significant drag
Risk monitoring Real-time automated Manual / basic
SVI calibration Millisecond refresh Can build yourself
The practical path for a retail vol trader: focus on longer time horizons where speed doesn't matter, use public data (Deribit historical), backtest regime-based strategies, and size conservatively — the edge is real but thin.
